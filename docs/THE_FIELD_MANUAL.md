# LIVING SUPERWEAPON — THE FIELD MANUAL

*A complete guide to the game, written to be read aloud.*

---

## PART ONE — WHAT THIS GAME IS

Living Superweapon is a game about being a person who can level a city block, standing in a city
full of people who cannot.

You look down at it from above and at an angle, the way you'd look at a model on a table. The
camera doesn't sit behind your shoulder; it hangs over the street. That distance is deliberate. It
means you can always see the block you're about to destroy, and it means you can always see the
people standing in it.

The world is built at true human scale. Your character is nine and a half units tall, which is
about one metre eighty — a tall adult. A car is twenty-four units long and comes up to your chest.
A streetlight stands at thirty-two. Downtown towers rise between fifty-four and a hundred and
fifty, and the sky above them goes to three hundred and twenty before you hit the ceiling. When
you stand in the road next to a parked car, that car is the size a car should be. When you fly to
the top of a tower and look down, the drop is the drop.

That one decision — everything at one-to-one — is what makes the rest of it land. A thrown car is
heavy because it's a real car. A crowd is frightening because they're real people at real size.
And when you punch someone through a wall, the wall was a real wall.

Underneath all of it there is a data layer most games would never bother with. There are one
thousand and fifty real cities in this game, each one carrying its true population, its city
types, a crime index and a safety index, and a code describing which part of the world it
architecturally belongs to. Behind those cities sit one hundred and sixty-eight real countries,
each carrying how good its police are, how well funded they are, how clean its government is, how
free its press is, and — critically — whether people like you are legal there at all.

The city decides what the fight looks like. The country decides what happens to you afterwards.

## PART TWO — THE BODY. HOW YOU MOVE.

### Walking, and the double tap

You move with the left stick, or with W A S D. Your character walks and runs the way you'd expect,
and turns to face where they're going, with a little smoothing so they lean into a direction change
rather than snapping to it.

Hold the dash button — shift, or the right shoulder — and you surge forward.

But the important movement isn't the dash. It's the **evade**, and you get to it by tapping a
direction *twice, quickly*. Two taps inside about a quarter of a second and your character performs
their personal escape move. Every character has a different one, and it is one of the clearest ways
they differ from each other.

Most fighters **dash** — a hard, fast slide in the direction you tapped.

Teleporters **blink** — they vanish and reappear a short distance away with no travel in between,
which means nothing can hit them on the way.

Some characters **sprint**, which is less an escape than a burst of raw speed.

RIME **slides**. She's a cryokinetic and she skates — her evade has almost no friction, so she
keeps going long after anyone else would have stopped. Learning to steer that slide is learning
to play her.

And SPECTER **phases**. Their evade turns them briefly intangible, and the window of invulnerability
is longer than anyone else's. It is the single best defensive movement in the game.

The bots evade too. If you throw something slow at a competent opponent, expect them to juke it.

### Flight

Flight is a **mode**, not a button you hold. You press F — or the cross button on a pad — and now
you are flying. You press it again and you are not. This matters more than it sounds, because the
old way round meant that any knockback that bumped you to the ground silently switched flight off,
and that read as the game breaking.

While flying: hold up to rise, release to **hover**, hold down to descend. Hovering is a real state
— you sit at your altitude with a gentle bob rather than sinking. There's a soft floor built in, so
if you drift low you'll float rather than scrape your ankles along the pavement.

Hold the dash button while flying and you **cruise** — half again as fast, at the cost of a trickle
of energy per second. Past a certain speed you'll see speed lines. That's how you cross a city.

**Not everyone can fly, and the differences are the point.**

Some characters are **grounded** entirely. SARGE and GALE are human beings with equipment; pressing
the flight button does nothing but refuse. They leap instead.

Some are **clumsy fliers** — VOLT, HIVE, KRAKEN. They can get airborne, but they can't hover. Let
go and they sag. They drift on a sine wave. Flying as them feels like being bad at flying, which is
exactly right.

Some are **levitators** — RIME, WARDEN, PYRE, RIFT, TITAN, KIVULI. They move through the air at
about two-thirds speed. They reposition; they don't cruise.

And some are **full fliers**. They own the sky.

Two of them fly with style. TORCH and PYRE leave a wake of fire. RIME rides an actual board of ice
that forms under her feet.

The way a flying body is posed is worth describing, because a lot of care went into it. Your
character aligns with the direction they are *travelling*, not the direction they are facing. In
level cruise you go prone and head-first, one fist punched out past your head and the other arm
swept back, legs trailing. Rising, you go vertical — head pointing where you're going. Hovering
still, you're fully upright. Diving, you're nose down. Strafing, you bank into the turn. And how
committed that pose is scales with your speed, so drifting slowly keeps you upright and dignified
while a full-speed cruise puts you flat out like a bullet.

### The ground marker — the most useful thing on screen

Under every single fighter, yours and theirs, there is a marker on the ground. It is doing three
jobs at once and once you learn to read it you will never stop.

The **outer ring is coloured by altitude band.** Green means they're on the ground. Gold means
they're up at building height. Cyan means sky. White means clouds. If you want to reach someone,
match their colour.

The **bright wedge at the front of the ring is where they are looking.** Not where their body is
turned — where they are actually aiming. This is the tell that makes flanking possible, because you
can see the exact moment someone's attention leaves you.

And the **inner ring changes colour for what they are doing.** Blue means they're guarding. Green
means they're going for a grab. Orange means they are winding up a haymaker, and it grows as the
wind-up charges — that's your window to move. White means they've committed to a strike. Red means
they're staggered, which means it's your turn.

Learn those five colours and you can read a fight from across the block.

## PART THREE — THE FIST. HOW YOU FIGHT.

### The triangle

Melee is a triangle, and it is strict.

**Strike beats Grab. Grab beats Guard. Guard beats Strike.**

You **strike** with V, or square. You **grab** with G, or circle. You **guard** by holding C — or
X, or a side mouse button, or the left shoulder, depending on your control scheme.

If you get hit while starting a grab, your grab is cancelled — that's Strike beating Grab. A grab
ignores a raised guard completely — that's Grab beating Guard. And a strike into a raised guard
gets you hurt — that's Guard beating Strike, and it's worth its own section.

### Striking, and the haymaker

The strike button is pressure-sensitive in time. **Tap it** and you throw a jab, and jabs combo.
**Hold it briefly** and — if your character has three melee tiers — you throw a straight. **Hold it
past about half a second** and you throw a **HAYMAKER**.

A haymaker's damage scales with how long you charged it *and* with your Strength. It also winds you
up visibly — remember that orange ring — so a good opponent knows it's coming.

Throw it into a raised guard anyway. Because a haymaker does something no other attack does: it
**crushes the guard.** The blocker is staggered for the better part of a second, loses over half
their guard meter, and is wide open. That's the answer to a turtle.

### The block law — why spamming doesn't work

This is the most important rule in the melee system, and it exists because of a real problem.

There was a period where a fast character could simply hold down attack and win, because blocking
did nothing to stop them. It does now.

**Every blocked strike rejects the attacker.** They get bounced back thirty-eight units, staggered
for nearly half a second, and locked out of attacking. Their charge is cleared. Their combo window
is gone. Blocking is not a passive defence — it is a counter-attack that costs you nothing.

And if you time it — if you raise your guard in the last fifth of a second before the hit lands —
you get a **PARRY**. Bigger push, longer stagger, some of your meter refunded, a gold star and a
slice of slow motion.

That rule lives at a single choke point deep in the damage code, which means it covers *every*
melee source in the game — including melee-type abilities and rushing combos that used to sneak
past it. There is no attack in the game that is free against a raised guard.

Guarding does cost you. You move slower. Your guard meter drains as you absorb hits, and if it
empties you're staggered for seven-tenths of a second. But holding guard also charges your energy,
so a defensive stance is never wasted time.

### The three kinds of guard

Not everyone blocks the same way, and this is one of the bigger character differences.

Most characters have a plain **block** — a frontal shield that cuts incoming damage hard.

VANGUARD and TITAN have a **deflect** guard. Projectiles and arrows don't just stop — they *bounce
back at whoever fired them*. Shooting at them is a mistake.

AURUM and RIME have a **barrier**. It covers all three hundred and sixty degrees, so you cannot
flank it — but it burns sixteen energy per second, which is more than they regenerate. A barrier is
a clock. Wait it out, or drain them.

### Grabbing, and throwing the city

A grab seizes your opponent. Grab them from **behind** and they cannot escape, and it hurts more.

Some characters break the rules here. Ones with **thorns** hurt you for grabbing them. Ones with
**teleport escape** blink out automatically — but only from a *front* grab, which is why back-grabs
matter. Some **heal** off a grab.

KRAKEN is built entirely around it. He has four verlet-simulated tentacles that reach out, seize
you, drag you in, and slam you into the nearest wall.

And then there's the city itself. Press G near a car, a street tree or a lamp post and — if your
Strength is six or higher — you **pick it up**. Carrying slows you by about a third. Press G again
and you throw it along a visible arc.

That arc is drawn using the exact same maths the thrown object flies with, so the preview cannot
lie to you.

## PART FOUR — THE POWERS

Every character has a kit of abilities on the mouse buttons and Q, E, H and R. R is always the
ultimate. All of them spend **ki** — the blue bar — and if you run out mid-ability, the game tells
you loudly: your character fizzles, smoke comes off them, the bar pulses red and says DRAINED.

A few power types are worth understanding because they have rules of their own.

**Beams are hoses, not lasers.** A beam has a travelling tip that drags the beam behind it. You can
steer it. It is never instant, and that's a house rule — never make a beam hitscan.

Which means beams can **clash**. Two opposing beams meeting head-on form a struggle point that
slides toward whoever is weaker. Weakness is your might times your power buff times how much energy
you have left. Lose the struggle and the clash point reaches you and detonates.

**Charging means scale.** Hold a charge ability longer and the orb literally grows — bigger, more
damage, wider blast, harder ground shockwave, more lightning. What you see is what it will do.

**Crossing a power tier is a ceremony.** As you level, you pass through tiers — one through three
is tier one, four through six is tier two, seven through nine is tier three, ten is max. Crossing
one triggers a shockwave, lightning, a pillar of light, slow motion and an announcement. Your aura
shifts from your own colour toward gold and then to white-hot. And the meter panel on your HUD
physically widens, so a maxed meter *looks* bigger.

TITAN is the exception that proves it. He has an infinite core — his energy never runs out — and in
exchange his tier is hard-capped at two. That's the android trade.

## PART FIVE — DAMAGE, AND WHAT ACTUALLY HURTS WHAT

Every hit in this game carries a **type**, and every fighter carries resistances. This is the part
most players will never see written down, and it decides fights.

There are eight types.

**Physical** is fists, slams, and thrown cars. Almost nothing resists it. It's the honest baseline.

**Ballistic** is bullets, and it has its own scale. A bullet meets **armour** first — a flat
subtraction, with sparks off the plate — and then **toughness**, which scales with Strength. The
measured numbers tell the story: one shotgun blast does sixty damage to an unarmoured hero, about
eleven to RAGE, and *zero* through TITAN's plate. A gun is lethal to a person and an annoyance to a
superweapon.

**Energy** is ki — blasts and beams. The universal currency. Few things resist it.

**Fire** burns over time. Strong against flesh, weaker against metal.

**Cold** builds up **frost** until you are encased in solid ice — no actions, physics still shoves
you around. Strength melts you out faster. Fire-blooded characters shrug it off. A heavy hit
shatters the ice early for bonus damage.

**Toxic** is poison and gas — and it needs a metabolism. **A machine cannot be poisoned.** The
number will literally say IMMUNE.

**Acid** is the anti-armour type, and it's the counter to that ballistic scale. Acid **corrodes
plate** for five seconds. A chassis that was shrugging off shotgun blasts starts taking them: that
same nine-damage round goes from zero to nearly two while the corrosion holds. Against bare flesh
it's the wrong tool — it does thirty percent less. Carrying acid is a decision, not an upgrade.

**Magic** attacks the will, and it **siphons** — it pulls energy out of the target and into the
caster. It's resisted by Resolve, so an iron-willed fighter shrugs it off while a fragile one takes
more than baseline. Roughly half the roster is soft to it.

The rule that makes it a system rather than a pile of numbers: **a resistance has to cut both
ways.** Metal shrugs off poison and fire — and corrodes under acid. If something resists a type,
something else has to be weak to it, or it isn't a trade.

You can read all of this in-game. Open How To Play, then the **Damage Codex**. Every immune,
resistant and weak list on that screen is generated from the live combat tables, so it can never
lie to you about what the engine is doing.

## PART SIX — THE CITY IS ALIVE

### The people

Sixty-four civilians walk the streets on a real sidewalk grid. They walk in straight lines and turn
at intersections, the way people do.

When you get close, they stop and **film you**. You'll see the phone flashes.

When something explodes near them, they **scare** and run. When a blast catches them, they go
**down**, and that's a big deal.

### The villain law

**The villain is whoever hurts humans.** Not whoever you picked. Not whoever the story says.

Every civilian you knock down books **heat** on you. Enough heat and you are flagged: the HUD shows
a WANTED star rating, your name gets a VILLAIN tag, and the police come.

How fast they come is not a constant. It's read from real data. It starts with the city's safety
index, and then the country's police force is applied on top: how competent they are, how well
funded, and how clean the government is.

The measured spread: **Tokyo, four seconds. Hell, Norway, four seconds. Mexico City, nearly fifteen.
Mogadishu, nearly sixteen. Kabul, twenty-one.**

And in a country with low institutional integrity, sometimes **the call simply is not answered.** In
Kabul, better than a quarter of them aren't. You'll get a quiet line — *the call goes unanswered* —
and a long, quiet minute to finish what you started. In Tokyo, that never happens.

Cruisers drive in from the nearest dry edge with red and blue lights running, park, and deploy real
officers — actual fighters, with fixation set to you. They only fight the villain, and nobody who
kept their hands clean can even target a badge. Reinforcements arrive every sixteen seconds and
escalate to a special response unit at three stars. Stop being the villain and they stand down and
go home.

### The crowd fights back

This is where the country data gets personal.

Every country in this game has a stance on vigilantism: **Legal, Regulated, or Banned.**

Where it's **legal**, you're supposed to be here. Nobody films you like a criminal. Nobody draws.

Where it's **banned**, two things happen.

First, **every phone pointed at you is evidence.** Being filmed while you're already flagged pushes
the police response up. The crowd is what calls them. Around a dozen phones in and the game tells
you plainly: *they are filming you — the whole block is filming you.*

Second — and only if they have actually **seen you hurt someone** — some of them are carrying, and
they **draw**. About a third of witnesses to violence in a country that bans people like you will
pull a pistol and start shooting.

They will not kill you. A civilian pistol round meets the same ballistic scale as everything else,
and against a Might-nine frame like RAGE it does barely more than one point of damage. That isn't
the point. The point is that the street is answering back, every shot is noise the police can hear,
and a city that has outlawed you will physically shoot at you for proving why.

Hurt nobody and none of it happens, even where you're illegal. They'll just film.

### The city breaks

The ground is a real heightfield, and it deforms. Explosions crater it. Mining districts have
genuine open pits dug into the terrain, and the metro is a thirteen-unit-deep open trench you can be
knocked down into. All of it is real, standable ground — you can be punched into a hole and have to
climb out.

Buildings have health. They crack visibly, then shatter into tumbling debris with a dust cloud and a
mini-crater. Cars explode and chain into each other. Trees and lamp posts come down.

And the whole city is procedurally generated from that world sheet — thirteen-plus district types
with multiple variants each, laid out on a sectional grid: residential courts, corporate towers with
skybridges, industrial tank farms, military compounds with helipads, capitol domes, campuses,
temples, mineworks, container ports, resorts, stadiums, hospitals, markets, metro lines, and open
farmland for the villages and small towns, which are country, not miniature cities.

Every district knows what it's next to now. A military compound that borders another military
compound doesn't build a fence between them — it's one base. Where two streets meet, you'll find a
corner bodega with an awning and a lit sign, facing the junction.

## PART SEVEN — THE BROADCAST

There is a news crew in almost every match, and they are real objects in the world.

A camera operator in a navy jacket and red cap, with a shoulder rig and a blinking tally light. A
field reporter in a crimson blazer with a flagged microphone. A van parked at the curb.

They **chase the fight**. They pick vantage points with real line-of-sight checks, prefer to shoot
side-on, and will never stand down the axis of a beam. Their camera zooms based on how far apart the
fighters are. The footage sways, and it sways worse when the operator is running or frightened. Near
explosions make them duck. Close ones knock them flat — and the camera keeps rolling from the
pavement, tilted.

**The camera is real.** On a highlight — a knockout, a big hit, a building collapse, a car chain —
the game genuinely renders from the operator's point of view, stamps it with the broadcast package
including the actual in-world clock and the district name, and stores it as footage. Knockouts
record at twenty frames a second and carry a slow-motion window.

After the match, you watch the news. A TV set replays the real clips with static between them, the
anchor script types itself out, and there's a tale of the tape, a city damage desk, and a scrolling
ticker.

The script is written in **reporter register**, and that's a deliberate constraint. Civilians don't
know your move names. They describe what they saw. Your character is referred to by their civilian
identity — *registered to Samuel Ellison of Ellsworth, Kansas.* The desk detects comebacks and
shutouts. It estimates the damage in dollars. It quotes witnesses by district.

## PART EIGHT — READING THE SCREEN

**Bottom left** is you: health, energy, guard meter, your level badge and experience bar. Energy
turns amber below thirty-eight percent and pulses red below fifteen.

**Above that** are your ability chips, one per slot, with cooldowns shown as a numeric countdown and
a height-fill overlay.

**Top right** is the radar — the arena, cover blocks, your gold marker with a vision wedge, red dots
for enemies, and question marks where you last saw someone before they vanished.

**Right edge** is the altitude ladder — four rungs, ground, building, sky, clouds, with the live one
lit in your character's colour and your height in metres.

**Bottom right** is the controls panel. It shows for about eighteen seconds of a fresh match, then
folds into a corner chip. F1 brings it back and pins it. It's organised into groups now — move and
aim, melee, powers, flight, system — and it's generated from your *active control scheme*, so it can
never tell you the wrong key.

**Bottom centre** names the city you're fighting in.

When you're hit, red arcs appear at the screen edge pointing at whoever hit you. Below twenty-eight
percent health, the screen pulses red. Off-screen enemies get an edge arrow with their name and
distance — but only ones you can genuinely see, because pointing at someone hidden in fog would be
cheating.

## PART NINE — THE OPPONENTS

The AI in this game is built on two laws, and both were fixed because they were broken.

**The honesty law: a bot may only act on what it has earned.** There was a version where bots fell
back to your true position whenever their memory expired, which meant one that had never seen you
would still walk straight at you.

Now they have **belief**, not knowledge. They learn where you are three ways: **sight**, which is
exact and lasts four seconds; **radio**, when an ally with eyes on you calls it in, accurate to
about six units; and **noise** — explosions, solid hits, knockouts — which gives a fuzzy point that
gets fuzzier with distance.

When a lead goes cold they search it, then sweep the area, then patrol the district. And while
searching, **their eyes sweep** — which is exactly why holding still and juking actually work.

Verified: a hidden, quiet player was never found across five hundred units of map. Set off one
explosion and they investigate immediately. Break line of sight mid-fight and they lose you in under
three seconds and reacquire around nine.

**The fairness law: difficulty buys skill, never certainty.** Honest knowledge wasn't enough,
because they still had superhuman *hands*. Their aim used to snap a hundred and eighty degrees in a
single frame.

Now they have a **finite turn rate** — the head is a neck. They have **imperfect aim** that drifts
on a slow random walk rather than jittering. They **lead** moving targets imperfectly. And they have
a **reaction time** that gates both noticing you and defending, so feints and fast openers genuinely
work.

Measured: circle-strafing leaves a bot about four degrees behind on average — they'll still kill you
— but tight orbits open gaps of a hundred and seventy degrees. Flanking is real.

## PART TEN — HOW TO WIN

**Read the ground marker.** Colour tells you altitude, wedge tells you where they're looking, inner
ring tells you what they're doing. Orange means a haymaker is coming.

**Block, don't dodge, against melee.** A blocked strike bounces the attacker off you for free. If
they keep swinging into your guard, you win by standing still.

**Haymaker the turtles.** It's the only thing that crushes a guard.

**Get behind people.** Back grabs cannot be escaped and hit harder.

**Match altitude.** You cannot punch someone in the sky. Match their ring colour first.

**Bring the right damage.** Acid against armour. Never poison against a machine — it does literally
nothing. Magic against the weak-willed.

**Watch their energy, not just their health.** Barrier guards drain themselves. Beam duels are
decided by who has more left.

**Break line of sight.** Bots genuinely lose you. Quiet and hidden beats fast and loud.

**Keep your hands clean — or don't, and mean it.** Hurting civilians makes you the villain, and in
the wrong country the crowd films you, the police come faster, and some of them start shooting. That
is a choice the game will let you make. It just won't let you make it quietly.

## APPENDIX — CONTROL SCHEMES

Three layouts, switchable in Options, and the help panel always shows the one you're using.

**CLASSIC** — what shipped. The mouse wheel and the number keys swap characters. Z descends. C
guards. X is your gadget.

**PILOT** — the wheel picks which power you fire with the left mouse button. Character swap moves to
the square brackets. Space rises, C descends, X guards, Z is the gadget.

**HYBRID** — Pilot's wheel and brackets, but guard and gadget stay on the old C and X.

On a controller: sticks move and aim, the triggers fire your two main powers, square and circle are
strike and grab, the left shoulder guards, cross flies, triangle is your ultimate, and the d-pad
covers your remaining abilities. Start pauses, select opens the roster.

In menus, the d-pad or left stick moves a gold focus ring, A confirms, B goes back. The whole game —
every roster screen, every panel, the pause menu, and quitting — is drivable without ever touching a
mouse or a keyboard.
